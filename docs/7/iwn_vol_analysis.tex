\section{Executive Summary}
% ============================================================

This report describes a systematic scalping strategy where a single volatile
long position is held at a target allocation and periodically scalped against
correlated put hedges. Free cash from scalp trades funds limit orders for puts
priced with slight buffers against current momentum, recapturing transaction
costs during momentum-based crossovers. Correlations, volatility pricing, and
take-profit targets are periodically revisited, with manual intraday
rebalancing and risk-based sell-offs to cover strategy gaps.

As of March~4, 2026, this portfolio maintains a 46.1\% BTC Mini Trust
position with \textcolor{gain}{+\$15,770} in BTC trading P\&L and
\textcolor{gain}{+\$4,195} in options P\&L over the Feb~21--Mar~4 window.
The BTC Mini Trust position has been held within 10\% of the target
${\raise.17ex\hbox{$\scriptstyle\sim$}}$50\% portfolio allocation during the
entirety of the Dec~4, 2025 to March~4, 2026 trading window. This report
documents live trading results alongside full-history backtests, correlation
analysis, and volatility pricing methodologies.

\subsection{Rolling Return Correlations}

In the midst of recent market volatility, BTC Mini Trust has exhibited

\textbf{During BTC sell-offs, IWN tends to sell off in tandem, making IWN puts a
good proxy hedge for BTC drawdowns.} This criteria can be summarised as:

\begin{enumerate}[itemsep=4pt]
  \item Institutional demand for IWN and IWN options are less when compared
        to SPY puts. This simplifies the relevant macro landscape during
        research.
  \item As of March~4, 2026, AI-driven uncertainty increases small-cap
        volatility; IWN serves as a strong diversified basket to simplify
        momentum, manage drawdown events, and trade sentiment.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Volatility pricing and signal generation} for these pairs can be summarised as:

\begin{enumerate}[itemsep=4pt]
  \item BTC is a good proxy for news-events, with weekend movements
        providing some signal for hedging targets during the weekday.
  \item Implied and realised volatility for both tend to change frequently
        across a 30-day window, providing good exit points for option
        positions at fair prices.
  \item Large drawdowns in BTC generally happen in tandem with indexes,
        allowing puts to buffer a large portion of unrealised losses while
        profitability is generated via scalping.
  \item Breaks in the correlation (i.e.\ BTC increases while IWN drops, or

\section{Implied Volatility Smile}
% ============================================================

The \textbf{implied volatility smile} (or ``smirk'') is one of the most
important features of equity options markets. It describes how implied
volatility varies across strikes for a given expiration, deviating from the
constant-vol assumption of the Black-Scholes model.

\subsection{Theory}

In the Black-Scholes framework, all options on the same underlying with the
same expiration should have identical implied volatility regardless of strike.
In practice, this is never observed. Instead:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Equity index options} exhibit a \textit{volatility skew}
        (also called a ``smirk''): OTM puts have higher IV than ATM options,
        which in turn have higher IV than OTM calls. This reflects demand for
        downside protection and the empirical fat-tailed, negatively-skewed
        distribution of equity returns.
  \item \textbf{Single-stock options} may show a more symmetric smile,
        especially around earnings events where large moves in either
        direction are expected.
  \item \textbf{Small-cap ETFs like IWN} tend to exhibit a pronounced skew
        similar to broad indices, as institutional hedging demand concentrates
        in OTM puts.
\end{itemize}

The smile can be characterised by its level (ATM IV), slope (the put-call
skew), and curvature (how quickly IV increases away from ATM in both
directions). Formally, for a fixed expiry $T$:
\[
  \sigma_{\mathrm{imp}}(K) = \sigma_{\mathrm{ATM}} + b_1\left(\frac{K - S}{S}\right)
  + b_2\left(\frac{K - S}{S}\right)^2 + \cdots
\]
where $b_1 < 0$ captures the skew (puts more expensive) and $b_2 > 0$
captures the curvature (both wings elevated).

\subsection{Interpreting the Smile for Hedging}

The IV smile has direct implications for the put hedging strategy:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Skew cost}: OTM put protection costs more (in IV terms) than
        ATM. For IWN, the put-call IV skew adds
        ${\raise.17ex\hbox{$\scriptstyle\sim$}}$2--5pp to OTM put IV relative
        to OTM calls, increasing hedge cost.
  \item \textbf{ATM vs OTM trade-off}: ATM puts provide better vol-adjusted
        value (lower IV) but cost more in dollar terms. OTM puts are cheaper
        in dollars but embed a skew premium.
  \item \textbf{Vega risk}: When the smile steepens (skew increases), existing
        put positions gain value even without a move in the underlying. This
        creates a secondary source of P\&L beyond pure delta hedging.
  \item \textbf{Calendar spread opportunity}: if near-term IV is elevated
        relative to longer-dated IV (inverted term structure), selling
        short-dated puts and buying longer-dated puts captures the term
        structure normalisation.
\end{itemize}
